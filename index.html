For Hospital Clients & Steering Committees

Your hospital project deserves
institutional thinking.

A plain-language guide to what really matters when selecting your design and delivery team — and what most clients never think to ask.

01

A hospital is not a building. It is an operational system.

Most failed hospital projects do not fail because of bad architecture. They fail because the architecture, engineering, fire safety, medical planning, and construction teams were never truly coordinated. The most expensive moment in any hospital project is discovering in Month 18 that the HVAC ducts and fire sprinkler mains cannot occupy the same ceiling zone.

Every approval delay, every NABH rejection, every post-construction retrofit traces back to a coordination void that was present in the design documentation — invisible until it became unavoidable.

What breaks silently

MEP clashes, NABH documentation gaps, and coordination voids appear only after concrete is poured — and cost exponentially more to fix.

What gets delayed

Fire NOC, KPME licensing, and AERB approvals all require documentation that starts at design stage, not at project completion.

What protects you

One accountable coordination authority with proven hospital delivery experience prevents most of these failures from occurring.

02

Three questions that matter more than any portfolio presentation.

Before selecting any consultant, ask these three questions directly. The quality and specificity of the answers will tell you more about a firm's true capability than any slideshow or photograph collection.

→
Has this firm built a hospital that has successfully received NABH accreditation after completion — and can they show you the documentation they produced to achieve it, not just photos of the finished building?
→
How many RFIs (Requests for Information) were raised on their last hospital project, and what was the average resolution time? High RFI volume is a documentation failure, not a construction one.
→
Ask them to describe, in detail, how they would coordinate an ICU pressure-zoning conflict between the HVAC consultant and the infection-control planner. Superficial answers reveal real depth.
03

The consultant fee is the smallest number in this decision.

A hospital project typically spends 3–5% of its capital budget on all consultant fees combined. The coordination failures that those fees either prevent or allow to happen can cost 10–25% of the capital budget in retrofits, rework, delays, and lost revenue days from deferred operations.

Choosing the lowest-fee consultant ecosystem is not cost management. It is risk accumulation with a deferred payment date.

04

Red flags to watch for during presentations.

No NABH vocabulary

Consultants who don't spontaneously use terms like NABH, KPME, AERB, MGPS, or NBC likely lack healthcare documentation depth.

Solo architect model

A single firm offering to manage all hospital systems coordination independently, without named specialist partners, is a high-risk appointment.

Portfolio-only defense

Firms who respond to technical coordination questions with portfolio images rather than process descriptions are showing you what they lack, not what they have.

Fee significantly below market

Below-market consultant fees usually signal inadequate documentation bandwidth or understaffed delivery — not efficiency.
