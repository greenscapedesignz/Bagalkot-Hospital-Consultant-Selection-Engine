01

A hospital is not a building. It is an operational system.

Most failed hospital projects do not fail because of bad architecture. They fail because the architecture, engineering, fire safety, medical planning, and construction teams were never truly coordinated. The most expensive moment in a hospital project is discovering in Month 18 that the HVAC ducts and fire sprinklers cannot occupy the same ceiling zone.

What breaks silently

MEP clashes, NABH documentation gaps, and coordination voids appear only after concrete is poured.

What gets delayed

Fire NOC, KPME licensing, and AERB approvals all require documentation that starts at design, not at completion.

What protects you

One accountable coordination authority with proven hospital delivery experience prevents most of these failures.

02

Three questions that matter more than any portfolio.

Before selecting any consultant, ask yourself these three things. The answers will tell you more than any presentation.

→
Has this firm built a hospital that has received NABH accreditation after completion — and can they show you the documentation they produced to achieve it, not just photos of the building?
→
How many RFIs were raised on their last hospital project, and what was the average resolution time? High RFI volume is a documentation failure, not a construction one.
→
Ask them to describe, in detail, how they would coordinate an ICU pressure-zoning conflict between the HVAC consultant and the infection-control planner. Process answers reveal real depth.
03

The fee is the smallest number in this decision.

A hospital project typically spends 3–5% of its capital budget on consultant fees. The coordination failures those fees either prevent or cause can cost 10–25% of the capital budget in retrofits, delays, and lost revenue days.

04

Red flags to watch for in presentations.

No NABH vocabulary

Consultants who don't spontaneously use terms like NABH, KPME, AERB, MGPS, or NBC may lack healthcare documentation depth.

Solo architect model

A single firm offering to manage all hospital systems coordination without named specialist partners is a significant red flag.

Portfolio-only defense

Firms who respond to technical coordination questions with portfolio images rather than process descriptions.

Fee significantly below market

Below-market fees often signal inadequate documentation bandwidth, not efficiency.
